Project Overview

In this project, we learned about the core aspects of physical simulation and worked on implementing a cloth simulation as it falls on various objects and itself, alongside adding various textures and designs to the cloth to make it look more visually interesting and unique.

In Part 1, our main goal was to build the grid of point masses and connect them with springs to form the main cloth mesh.

In Part 2, we implemented the physics equations to model the forces between the springs, and external forces to simulate the cloth at each time-step. We first computed the total force acting on each point mass using Hooke’s law and then used Verlet integration to compute the new position of the point mass for one time-step in the future. We used a technique proposed by Xavier Provot in order to constrain the position so that it is reasonable.

In Part 3, we programmed the position corrections to be applied when the cloth collides with an object like a sphere or a plane.

In Part 4, we implemented self-collisions which allow the cloth to fall and fold in on itself without clipping through itself.

In Part 5, we added shaders to the cloth in order to render cool textures and reflections on the cloth in a quick and efficient manner.

Through working on this assignment, we both were able to better understand how the various concepts we learned in class can be used in real-world applications through a hands-on project. It was really fun to play around with the various parameters in our simulations and see how our cloth would behave under different environments, as well as see the effects of these changes in real-time due to the simulation being extremely quick and efficient!

Describe the effects of changing the spring constant ks; how does the cloth behave from start to rest with a very low ks? A high ks?

With a very low ks, the cloth is much looser and more flexible. There are more folds in the cloth and the cloth appears more stretchy. When the ks is set to 50 N/m, there are many folds present at the top of the cloth after it drops compared to just the one major one with the default ks of 5,000 N/m. When the ks is set to 500 N/m there are still two folds at the top. With a high ks, the cloth appears to be more rigid and tight. The side folds present with the default ks of 5,000 N/m disappear when the ks is set to 10,000 N/m or greater. When the ks is set to 100,000 N/m, the fold at the top of the cloth almost goes away, the cloth almost appears to be a nearly vertical piece made out of a rigid material like plastic or wood.

What about for density?

A low density means that the cloth has less mass per unit area. This makes the cloth appear more light since the area of the cloth is the same. When the density was set to the smallest value of 1 g/cm^2 the cloth looks like a piece of paper being held at the top. The top shows no curvature and is flat compared to the default density of 10 g/cm^2 where the top bends downwards in a concave manner. The default density of 10g/cm^2 has the top bending down because the cloth is denser and therefore heavier causing the middle part to cave in. When the density is increased, the cloth appears heavier. When the density was set to 50 g/cm^2 the middle part of the cloth caved down a lot more than in the default density. When the density was increased all the way to 100,000 g/cm^2, the cloth is so artificially dense that there are no more folds and there is a very large cave in at the top.

What about for damping?

The damping does not affect the final state, but affects how much energy is lost due to friction. A smaller damping value means that the cloth flaps a lot more before eventually settling to its steady state value. A higher damping value means that the cloth barely flaps around and reaches its steady state value without as much fluctiation.

Varying Density

When varying the density of the cloth, it essentially acts to change the “heaviness” or “stiffness” of the cloth as it falls. This affects how it falls on itself since a cloth with lower density is much more light and stiff, so it tends to bounce and jiggle around a lot more as it falls down with very bends/wide folds, almost like a very thin sheet of jell-o falling on itself. On the other hand, a cloth with higher density feels much more dense and loose, and it tends to crumple in on itself much more without bouncing around much, and has much smaller bends/folds since it the cloth is less stiff overall.

Explain in your own words what is a shader program and how vertex and fragment shaders work together to create lighting and material effects.

Shader programs are essentially isolated programs that are designed to run in parallel across a GPU. This helps run real-time and interactive programs much faster, as these shader programs are essentially doing the job of executing different sections of the graphics pipeline in parallel.

For the scope of our project, we focus on working with two types of shader programs: vertex shaders and fragment shaders. As the names aptly suggest, vertex shaders are used to perform calculations and transformations on the vertices in our graphics, modifying properties like the position and normal vectors of our points in 3D space. Fragment shaders are used to process the fragments that are produced after rasterization, taking in the geometric data for each fragment after the vertex shader executes to compute and assign colors to each fragment.

Together, we can link both of these shaders so that the output of the vertex shader then feeds into the input of the fragment shader, and thus results in our lighting and material effects.

As shown below, we have implemented and rendered our sphere with both bump mapping and displacement mapping. In bump mapping, the approach is to modify the normal vectors of our object so that we can get an illusion of detail (aka the bumps) with the fragment shader. In displacement mapping, the approach is to additionally modify the vertex shader to change the position of vertices as well in order to reflect the height map. The latter (displacement mapping) results in physical changes to the actual shape instead of simply “appearing” to have details that are displayed through shading tricks. These details can be seen below in our sample images for both bump and displacement mapping:

When the mesh coarseness was set to 16 by 16, in the displacement mapping screenshot below, we can clearly see that the sphere has sharp edges and appears to be almost “lumpy”. In other words, it appears that there are many peaks and valleys on the sphere. When the mesh coarseness was increased to 128 by 128, in the displacement mapping screenshot below, the sphere has less jagged edges and appears more round in general. This is because when the coarseness is increased the differences in the texture are mapped to closer points, causing for fewer drastic changes in height.

There is no change in the bump mapping, since in the bump mapping we never change the physical locations of our vertices, while in displacement mapping we do.
