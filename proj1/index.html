Task 1: Drawing Single-Color Triangles

For Task 1, we implemented a rasterization algorithm for triangles which began by finding the smallest/tightest bounding box that could fully contain the triangle. Once this bounding box was defined, we iterated over each point within the box (with the offset of 0.5 in the x/y directions to ensure the center of each pixel was considered) and performed a 3-line test using a helper function called inside_triangle() to verify if the specific coordinate was within the triangle. If the point was determined to be inside the triangle, then we proceeded to rasterize it by calling the rasterize_point() function, which was responsible for assigning the corresponding color to the respective pixel in the sample buffer.

In terms of its effectiveness, our approach was equivalent to one which checked each sample within the triangle's bounding box, as that is exactly the method we followed. Therefore, the effectiveness of this approach is neither better nor worse than such an algorithm.

Task 2: Antialiasing by Supersampling

For Task 2, we implemented our supersampling algorithm in order to improve the overall image quality, by reducing aliasing and mitigating ‚Äújaggies‚Äù from our previous work in Task 1. The basic premise of supersampling is that is samples individual pixels at a higher rate than simple individual pixels, and thereafter aggregates data from higher sampling frequencies to produce a smoother blended overall picture at the original resolution.

The only data structures we needed to modify for this part was the sample_buffer. We increase the size by a factor of sample_rate in order to store the extra pixel information of our supersample. We do not modify the size of the frame buffer however, as that the final output should maintain the original size and resolution of our image as expected.

To accomplish our overall supersampling approach, we took the following steps and modifications to the rasterization pipeline:

  1. Given that the entire supersample is technically a factor of sample_rate larger than the original size, this means the width and height need to scale up by a factor of sqrt(sample_rate). As a result, we scale all the triangle coordinates in our rasterize_triangle() function by this factor .
  1. Thereafter, we iterate over these new larger-scale of pixels in the supersample (which is thus significantly more points than before at the original unscaled resolution). We proceed through the same process as Task 1 by searching over the triangle‚Äôs bounding box, performing the 3-line test to determine which points fall in the triangle, and assigning colors accordingly to the sample_buffer at our higher resolution.
  1. We also made slight modifications in the rasterize_point() and fill_pixel() functions, as singular points at the original resolution may now correspond to multiple points in the higher resolution supersampled sample_buffer. Since we don‚Äôt need to anti-alias points or lines (rasterize_point() is called by rasterize_line()), we thus modified these functions to simply fill in all nearby pixels in an area of sample_rate so they subsample properly in the final end result for the frame buffer.
  1. Given that the larger supersampling resolution does not match the original frame buffer resolution, we finally made modifications in the resolve_to_framebuffer() function to ‚Äúdownsample‚Äù our supersample back to the original output image resolution. To achieve this, we iterate over all pixels in a region area of sample_rate from the sample_buffer, and within this supersampled region we simply average out all the color values, and finally assign the original single pixel coordinate to that color in the framebuffer.

Based on the results, we can see that supersampling is useful because it significantly mitigates jaggies and aliasing for the colors, thus improving the overall appearance of the images to the viewer. The reason is works well is because it considers pixels at a finer granularity (higher sampling rate), so we can better assess not just simply whether a pixel is part of a triangle, but how much of that pixel is contained in the triangle. This allows us to beyond the simplistic binary color choice of an empty vs. colored pixel, and further assign a color intensity (brighter or darker hues) based on our supersampled understanding of the image. This allows for smoother transitions and gradients on the edges of our triangles, instead of sudden color changes like previously implemented which is very sharp and edgy. As you zoom out and look at the image as a whole, it thus appears much more seamless to the human eye.
